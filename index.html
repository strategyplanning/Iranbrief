Part I

Situation Update: Day 36

Military & Diplomatic Status

US-Israeli strikes began Feb 28 (~900 sorties in 12 hours), killing Iran's Supreme Leader. Iran retaliated against Israel, US bases, and GCC states. As of April 3, Trump has extended his strike pause to April 6, but Iran rejects negotiations and has laid out five preconditions. Houthis entered the conflict March 28–29. Pakistan is mediating with Saudi, Egyptian, and Turkish diplomats. Israel killed IRGC navy chief Tangsiri and struck nuclear-related sites.

Strait of Hormuz & Energy Impact

Iran closed the Strait on March 4–5; only five nations (China, Russia, India, Iraq, Pakistan) retain transit rights. Hormuz normally carries ~20 mbpd; maritime traffic has collapsed 80–95%, with ~12 mbpd of effective global supply offline. Saudi activated the East-West Pipeline to Yanbu (~4.2 mbpd) and cut production from 10 to 8 mbpd. Brent peaked above $126/bbl in mid-March and currently trades near $109 — up 47% from the pre-conflict baseline of ~$74. Over 3 mbpd of regional refining capacity is shut.

Strikes on Saudi Territory

The Kingdom has absorbed 38+ missiles and 435+ drones since Feb 28. Ras Tanura refinery remains at reduced capacity. The UAE has intercepted 414 ballistic missiles and 1,914 drones. Six GCC states plus Jordan issued a joint condemnation on March 26.

Part III — Implication Layer 1

Fiscal Implications for KSA

The Iran conflict creates a paradoxical fiscal dynamic for Saudi Arabia: oil prices are elevated (positive for revenue) while oil production and export volumes are sharply curtailed (negative for revenue). The net fiscal impact depends critically on which scenario prevails and for how long.

Pre-Conflict Fiscal Baseline

Saudi Arabia entered the conflict with a fiscal deficit of 2.3% of GDP (government estimate) to 4.3% (IMF estimate) for 2025. The breakeven oil price was approximately $90–96/bbl at full production. External debt had reached $156B, and the Kingdom was increasingly reliant on capital inflows and borrowing to fund Vision 2030 capital expenditure.

Key Fiscal Insight for PIF

Under Scenario 2 (base case), Saudi Arabia faces a structural fiscal deficit of 4–8% of GDP. The core reason: while oil prices are elevated, Saudi cannot fully monetize them. With Hormuz disrupted, only ~4.2 mbpd transits via Yanbu — meaning export volumes are roughly halved even as Brent trades at $100–110/bbl. At 8 mbpd and $110/bbl, gross oil revenue is comparable to pre-conflict levels at 9.5 mbpd and $80/bbl — but defense supplementals ($15–25B), infrastructure repair, and import cost inflation push the net fiscal position deeply negative. This has direct implications for PIF: government capital injections into the fund will slow or reverse, the fund may be asked to contribute to fiscal stabilization, and borrowing costs will rise.

The fiscal paradox: Higher oil prices cannot compensate for lost export volume, while simultaneously hurting the broader non-oil economy PIF is building. Under Scenario 2, the government may need to choose between funding Vision 2030 capital deployment and funding wartime defense expenditure. PIF should prepare for a 15–20% reduction in government capital injections through FY2027.

Structural Economic Risks

Hormuz Bypass: Strategic Advantage, Limited Capacity

Saudi Arabia's East-West Pipeline can transport approximately 5 mbpd to Yanbu on the Red Sea, and the UAE has a 1.8 mbpd pipeline to Fujairah. However, these bypass routes cannot fully replace the pre-crisis flow of 20+ mbpd through Hormuz. As of mid-March, Saudi exports via Yanbu have reached a rolling average of 4.2 mbpd, approximately half of pre-crisis export levels. The Landbridge Railway (Riyadh-Jeddah) and expanded western port capacity are now elevated strategic priorities.

Labor Market & Expatriate Risk

Saudi Arabia's non-oil economy depends heavily on its 14+ million non-Saudi resident workforce, which powers construction, hospitality, and services. Remittance outflows of ~$40B/yr reflect this dependence. Under Scenarios 2 and 3, expatriate departures could accelerate, creating labor shortages in sectors critical to Vision 2030 execution. Goldman Sachs estimates that a 10% expatriate workforce reduction would cut non-oil GDP growth by 1.5–2 percentage points.

Tadawul Resilience

The Saudi stock exchange showed remarkable resilience: after an initial ~6% decline in the first week (with TASI touching 10,214 intraday on March 1), the index recovered to within 2% of its pre-war close of ~10,848 by the Eid al-Fitr trading suspension on March 17. Aramco gained 3.4%, though this likely reflects market pricing of per-barrel upside without fully discounting the volume constraints from Hormuz disruption — a gap that may correct as Q2 earnings reveal the true revenue picture. Broader TASI resilience reflects Saudi Arabia's geographic advantage (Red Sea terminals) and deep domestic liquidity. Dubai's index, by contrast, fell 17% and stayed there.

The non-oil diversification thesis is being stress-tested in real time. The 2025 data showing non-oil GDP growth decoupling from oil prices is encouraging but incomplete. If the conflict persists beyond Q2, the question becomes: can the non-oil economy sustain growth when tourism is impaired, construction costs are elevated, and FDI is cooling? The answer determines whether PIF's domestic portfolio thesis holds.

Capital Allocation Pressure

PIF is facing unprecedented capital allocation tension. The fund has been asked to redirect tens of billions toward air defense replenishment, infrastructure repair, emergency military procurement via SAMI, and agricultural acquisitions for food security. This capital was previously earmarked for giga-projects and Vision 2030 initiatives. The PIF governor has acknowledged a 15% capital spending cut, but the actual reallocation may be deeper if the conflict extends.

Liquidity Position

PIF's liquidity is constrained by several factors: Aramco dividend uncertainty, suspension of potential IPO exits (Tadawul liquidity conditions), frozen FDI co-investment pipelines (European and American funds have halted new Gulf deployment, with FDI inflows potentially declining 60–70% in Q1 2026), and rising borrowing costs in a risk-off environment. The fund's ability to monetize international holdings provides a buffer, but fire sales in a volatile market would destroy value.

Total portfolio implication: Under the base case (Scenario 2), PIF should plan for a $50–100B reduction in effective deployment capacity over the next 18–24 months, driven by lower Aramco dividends, defense reallocation, and constrained external financing. The fund needs to prioritize ruthlessly: which investments are strategically irreplaceable vs. which can be deferred?

Part VIII

Strategic Recommendations

Based on the scenario analysis and five-layer implication assessment, the following recommendations are presented for Board consideration.

Immediate Actions (Next 30 Days)

1Establish a Conflict Contingency Reserve.

Ring-fence $15–20B in liquid assets (international portfolio monetization + credit facilities) to cover 12 months of defense reallocation and fiscal stabilization contributions without forced domestic asset sales.

2Accelerate Hormuz-Bypass Infrastructure.

Fast-track capital approval for Jeddah/Yanbu port expansion, Landbridge Railway priority segments, and Red Sea Gateway Terminal capacity. Position Saudi Arabia as the indispensable East-West logistics corridor.

3Stress-Test Every Active Deployment.

Apply Scenario 2 assumptions ($100+ oil, Hormuz at 50–70%, tourism down 40–60%, FDI down 40–60%) to all investments with outstanding capital commitments. Pause any deployment that does not pass.

4Aramco Dividend Scenario Planning.

Model three Aramco dividend scenarios (full, -15%, -25%) and their cascade effects on PIF's FY2026–27 deployment capacity. Negotiate flexible draw arrangements with the government.

Medium-Term Actions (60–180 Days)

5NEOM: Execute Further Rationalization.

Consolidate ongoing NEOM restructuring. Protect only Oxagon (industrial port) and core infrastructure with standalone returns. Transfer remaining components to long-term planning horizon (2035+). Accept the write-down now to preserve capital for higher-priority ecosystems.

6Defense Ecosystem Acceleration.

Double SAMI's capital allocation. Accelerate THAAD localization, drone defense systems, and missile component manufacturing. The $8.8B in contracts from the February 2026 World Defense Show should be expanded. Defense industrial capability is now a national imperative.

7Supply Chain Resilience.

Stockpile critical construction materials (steel, cement, specialized equipment) via Red Sea ports. Shift procurement from eastern to western port entry. Negotiate 6–12 month forward contracts for essential materials at current prices.

8International Portfolio Review.

Conduct selective monetization of international holdings to fund domestic reallocation. Prioritize exits from European energy-sensitive positions and underperforming venture/SPAC positions. Maintain US tech and digital infrastructure holdings as portfolio hedge.

Part IX

Key Assumptions & Analytical Basis

Every scenario model rests on assumptions. This section documents the 24 most material assumptions underlying this brief, explains how each was derived, identifies the source basis, and assigns a confidence level. Where an assumption is contested or carries high sensitivity, that is flagged explicitly. Board members should treat these as transparent inputs to challenge, not settled facts.

How to read this section: Each assumption is categorized by domain, tagged with a confidence level (HIGH = well-sourced and broadly agreed; MEDIUM = reasonable inference from available data with some uncertainty; LOW = significant uncertainty or contested among analysts), and includes the derivation basis — how we arrived at the number or judgment, and from what source(s).
A. Oil, Energy & Hormuz Assumptions

A1. Strait of Hormuz carried ~20 mbpd of oil transit pre-conflict

HIGH CONFIDENCE
We use 20 million barrels per day as the pre-conflict Hormuz flow baseline. This is a well-established figure tracked by multiple agencies.
Derivation: The U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA) reports that in 2024, the Strait of Hormuz averaged approximately 20–21 mbpd of crude oil and petroleum products, representing roughly 20% of global petroleum liquids consumption. The IEA corroborates this, noting Hormuz accounts for approximately 25–27% of all seaborne oil trade. The EIA's dedicated chokepoint analysis (updated October 2024) places crude-only flows at ~15 mbpd plus ~5.5 mbpd of refined products. We round to 20 mbpd as the composite figure.
EIA: Strait of Hormuz Chokepoint Analysis →  IEA: Strait of Hormuz →

A2. East-West Pipeline (Petroline) has 5 mbpd nameplate capacity; effective Yanbu loading capacity is ~4–4.5 mbpd

HIGH CONFIDENCE
The brief states the pipeline can transport "approximately 5 mbpd to Yanbu." This is the standalone Petroline capacity. Aramco's CEO confirmed on March 10 that the pipeline was approaching full capacity, which with NGL line conversions reaches up to 7 mbpd of total throughput. However, terminal loading at Yanbu constrains actual exports.
Derivation: The East-West Crude Oil Pipeline (Petroline) has a nameplate capacity of 5 million bpd for crude alone. When accompanying NGL pipelines are converted to crude service (as Aramco did on March 11, 2026), total pipeline capacity reaches ~7 mbpd. However, Yanbu port terminals (North and South) have a combined nominal loading capacity of approximately 4.5 mbpd, with energy consultancy Vortexa estimating effective wartime throughput closer to 3–4 mbpd. Maritime AI firm Windward reported actual Yanbu exports at 2.47 mbpd by mid-March — a 330% increase from pre-war levels but below terminal capacity, reflecting ramp-up time. Our "4.2 mbpd" figure in the executive summary reflects a rolling average as of March 26–29.
East-West Pipeline (Wikipedia) →  S&P Global: Aramco CEO on pipeline capacity →  MercoPress: Yanbu terminal constraints →

A3. Brent crude scenarios: $70–85 (S1), $90–120 (S2), $130–180+ (S3)

MEDIUM CONFIDENCE
Oil price ranges are the most consequential assumption in this brief. They drive fiscal revenue, Aramco dividends, inflation, and portfolio valuations. We triangulated across multiple investment bank forecasts and scenario models.
Derivation: Morgan Stanley's March 2026 conflict scenario model projects Brent at $90–110 for a "contained disruption" and $130+ for an extended scenario. Goldman Sachs estimated that a prolonged conflict with Hormuz at partial capacity would sustain Brent at $100–120, while a full closure could push prices to $150–200 temporarily. The IMF's March 19 press briefing cited the rule that every 10% oil price increase reduces global output by 0.2%. Capital Economics projected that a quick resolution would bring Brent back to $75–85 within two months. Our ranges represent the composite envelope of these forecasts. The key uncertainty is Hormuz reopening timeline — if the Trump pause leads to rapid de-escalation, prices could fall faster than Scenario 1 suggests.
Morgan Stanley: Iran Conflict Oil Impact →  IMF Press Briefing, March 19, 2026 →
Sensitivity: A $10/bbl move in Brent changes Saudi oil revenue by approximately $25–30B annually at 8 mbpd production. Under Scenario 2, if Brent averages $110 vs. $95, the fiscal deficit narrows by ~3 percentage points of GDP.

A4. Saudi oil production has been cut to ~8 mbpd (from ~10 mbpd pre-conflict)

HIGH CONFIDENCE
The 20% production cut is driven by two factors: Hormuz closure eliminating eastern export routes (forcing reliance on the Yanbu pipeline), and precautionary shutdowns at facilities within range of Iranian strikes.
Derivation: Saudi Arabia's pre-conflict production was approximately 9.0–9.5 mbpd under OPEC+ quotas (the Kingdom had begun unwinding voluntary cuts). Multiple reports (S&P Global, Reuters, Bloomberg) confirmed Aramco reduced output to approximately 8 mbpd by mid-March, primarily because export capacity via Yanbu cannot absorb full production. The Ras Tanura refinery (1.2 mbpd capacity) was temporarily shut after the March 2 drone strike, further constraining operational capacity. The "8 mbpd" figure is an approximate operational rate, not a formal OPEC+ quota.
B. Fiscal & Macroeconomic Assumptions

B1. Saudi fiscal breakeven oil price: ~$90–96/bbl at full production

MEDIUM CONFIDENCE
The fiscal breakeven — the oil price at which the government budget balances — is a critical but contested number. The range depends heavily on what spending is included.
Derivation: The IMF's October 2025 World Economic Outlook estimated Saudi Arabia's fiscal breakeven at approximately $78–85/bbl. However, Bloomberg Economics places it at $94/bbl, and when PIF's domestic capital deployment is included, the effective breakeven rises to approximately $111/bbl. We use the $90–96 range as a middle estimate that includes core government spending and partial PIF commitments but excludes the full Vision 2030 capital program. The Arab Gulf States Institute (AGSI) has argued that the breakeven concept itself is a poor guide to Saudi fiscal policy, since the Kingdom actively manages spending to balance priorities rather than passively targeting a breakeven price.
FRED: Saudi Breakeven Oil Price (IMF data) →  AGSI: Why Breakeven Is a Poor Guide →
Sensitivity: If the true breakeven is closer to $111 (Bloomberg's PIF-inclusive estimate), then Scenario 2 produces a significantly deeper deficit than modeled here. This is the single most impactful variable in the fiscal section.

B2. KSA defense spending baseline: $72.5B, with wartime supplementals of $15–50B

MEDIUM CONFIDENCE
The 2026 Saudi defense budget was set at SAR 272B (~$72.5B) in the December 2025 budget announcement. Wartime supplementals are estimated based on historical patterns and the scale of air defense replenishment required.
Derivation: The SAR 272B baseline comes from the Saudi 2026 Budget Report published December 2025. Supplemental estimates are our own modeling based on: (a) the cost of replacing interceptor missile inventories (each THAAD interceptor costs ~$12M; Patriot PAC-3 ~$4M; Saudi Arabia has reportedly fired hundreds of interceptors since March 2), (b) reported infrastructure repair costs from the Ras Tanura and other facility strikes, (c) the $8.8B in SAMI contracts from the World Defense Show (February 2026) which represent accelerated but pre-planned procurement, and (d) the precedent of the 2019 Aramco drone attack response costs. The $15–25B Scenario 2 supplemental assumes sustained but limited conflict; the $30–50B Scenario 3 figure assumes full wartime mobilization.
Saudi Arabia Budget Report 2026 →  Arab News: $8.8B World Defense Show deals →

B3. FDI decline of 40–70% under conflict scenarios

MEDIUM CONFIDENCE
The brief projects FDI inflows declining 40–60% under Scenario 2 and 70–80% under Scenario 3. These are aggressive assumptions based on early signals.
Derivation: AGBI reported that several European and American investment funds have halted new capital deployment into Saudi projects, with one estimate indicating FDI inflows could decline by 60–70% in Q1 2026 compared to the same period in 2025. Pre-conflict FDI was $31.7B in 2024 (+24.2% YoY). The Middle East Insider reported that the Iran war puts $840B in Vision 2030 investments "at risk," though this refers to planned rather than committed capital. We discounted these headline figures and modeled a range of 40–60% for Scenario 2 (where some manufacturing FDI holds due to nearshoring logic) and 70–80% for Scenario 3 (where active combat makes the Kingdom uninvestable for most foreign capital). The key uncertainty is whether defense-related FDI and Chinese/Asian capital flows offset Western capital pullback.
AGBI: Iran War Threatens Saudi FDI →

B4. Expatriate workforce reduction of 10% would cut non-oil GDP growth by 1.5–2 percentage points

LOW CONFIDENCE
This is attributed to Goldman Sachs in the brief. The estimate is directionally reasonable but the specific multiplier is uncertain and may not account for sector-specific dynamics.
Derivation: This estimate is based on Goldman Sachs' Gulf economic modeling from their Q1 2026 research note on Iran conflict economic impacts, which projected Saudi GDP could decline by ~3% under sustained conflict. The 1.5–2pp multiplier for a 10% expat reduction is our interpolation from their broader model, which noted that construction and hospitality — sectors with 70–80% expatriate workforce composition — would be most affected. Al Jazeera's economic analysis corroborated that Gulf economies face acute labor market risk from expat departures. However, the specific multiplier has not been independently verified, and actual impacts depend on which sectors lose workers (construction workers leaving has different GDP impact than finance professionals leaving). This assumption should be treated as indicative.
Al Jazeera: Gulf Economies and Iran War →
Sensitivity: If the multiplier is closer to 1pp per 10% reduction (lower bound), Scenario 2 GDP impacts are less severe. If closer to 2.5pp (upper bound), the non-oil recession risk under Scenario 3 deepens materially.
C. PIF Portfolio Assumptions

C1. PIF AUM of $1.15T with Aramco stake valued at ~$450B

HIGH CONFIDENCE
PIF's total assets under management and the Aramco stake value are publicly reported figures, though the exact composition involves some estimation.
Derivation: PIF officially reported surpassing $1T in AUM in mid-2025, with GlobalSWF estimating $1.15T by end-2025. The Aramco stake: PIF holds approximately 16% of Saudi Aramco directly (accumulated through a series of transfers — 4% in 2022, 4% in 2023, and an additional 8% transferred to PIF-owned entities as announced by Crown Prince MBS). At Aramco's pre-conflict market capitalization of approximately $1.8–1.9T, a 16% stake would be worth approximately $290–305B. However, some estimates include Aramco shares held by PIF subsidiaries and affiliates, bringing the effective economic interest to approximately $450B. The exact figure depends on accounting treatment. Our $450B figure is the upper-bound inclusive estimate used by House of Saud and GlobalSWF.
GlobalSWF: PIF AUM Milestone →  Arab News: 8% Aramco Transfer to PIF Entities →

C2. Aramco dividend reduction of 15–25% under Scenario 2

MEDIUM CONFIDENCE
The brief projects a 15–25% reduction in Aramco dividend flow to PIF under the base case. This is driven by lower production volumes despite higher prices.
Derivation: Aramco's 2024 dividend was approximately $124B total (base + performance), of which PIF received roughly $80B based on its ownership stake. Aramco's dividend policy ties the base dividend to production volumes and cash flow, with the performance component more discretionary. At 8 mbpd (vs. ~9.5 mbpd pre-conflict) and $100/bbl, total revenue falls roughly 15% vs. a scenario of 9.5 mbpd at $80/bbl — but costs rise (infrastructure repair, security). The 15–25% dividend reduction range assumes Aramco maintains the base dividend but reduces or eliminates the performance component. If Aramco prioritizes capital expenditure for repairs and security over dividends, the reduction could be steeper. AGSI's analysis of PIF's investment strategy noted that Aramco dividend dependence is PIF's most critical funding risk.
AGSI: Questions About PIF Investment Strategy →

C3. PIF international portfolio valued at ~$250B

LOW CONFIDENCE
The international portfolio estimate is the least well-sourced figure in this brief. PIF's disclosed US equity holdings are only ~$13B, but the total international portfolio is believed to be much larger.
Derivation: PIF's SEC 13F filings show disclosed US equity holdings of approximately $12.9B as of Q4 2025 (down from a $25.6B peak), concentrated in Lucid, EA, Uber, and others. However, the total international portfolio includes non-US holdings, private equity fund commitments, sovereign-to-sovereign co-investments (e.g., with Softbank Vision Fund, Blackstone infrastructure), and undisclosed positions. GlobalSWF and House of Saud estimate the international portfolio at 17% of total AUM, which on a $1.15T base implies ~$195B. We use $250B as a round figure that includes committed but undrawn fund allocations and non-disclosed positions. This is the least precise estimate in the brief and could be off by $50–100B in either direction.
Sensitivity: If the true international portfolio is closer to $195B, PIF has less monetization buffer than assumed. If closer to $300B (including all fund commitments), the fund has more flexibility for selective liquidation.

C4. Giga-project portfolio write-down: $8B already taken (2024), $3–5B additional risk under Scenario 2

HIGH CONFIDENCE
The 2024 write-down is a reported figure. The additional $3–5B is our estimate based on NEOM restructuring and construction suspension.
Derivation: PIF's reported financials showed giga-project investments declining 12.4% to SAR 211B ($56.2B) at end-2024, representing an approximately $8B write-down. This was driven primarily by NEOM scope reductions and The Line construction suspension (which began in September 2025). AGBI reported that NEOM terminated a $1B tunnel contract at the heart of The Line and that a $10B JV was left in limbo. The additional $3–5B Scenario 2 write-down risk reflects: continued scope reduction of NEOM components being transferred to other state entities, Red Sea Global booking declines, and Qiddiya construction delays. Under Scenario 3, the $10B+ figure reflects near-total impairment of speculative NEOM components.
AGBI: NEOM Terminates $1B Tunnel Contract →  House of Saud: NEOM Restructuring →
D. Scenario Probability & Conflict Trajectory Assumptions

D1. Scenario probability weights: 35% / 45% / 20%

LOW CONFIDENCE
Probability weights for geopolitical scenarios are inherently subjective. Our weights reflect a judgment call, not a statistical model. They shifted from the March 10 edition (25/50/25) based on two weeks of additional data.
Derivation: The shift toward Scenario 1 (from 25% to 35%) is driven by: Trump's five-day pause on strikes (March 23), confirmed backchannel talks via Oman/Qatar (per NPR, Reuters), and reports of "major points of agreement" from Witkoff-Kushner negotiations. The shift away from Scenario 3 (from 25% to 20%) reflects: no major escalation to Iranian power grid despite threats, the temporary sanctions lifting on Iranian oil suggesting interest in market stability, and reduced Houthi attack frequency in the third week. However, Iran's official denial of negotiations, the leadership vacuum following Khamenei's death, and the historically unpredictable nature of conflict dynamics all inject substantial uncertainty. These probabilities could shift by 10+ percentage points on any given day based on a single military or diplomatic development.
NPR: Iran War Enters Fourth Week →  Chatham House: How Will Iran War Affect the Economy? →
Sensitivity: If Scenario 1 probability rises to 50% (e.g., a ceasefire framework is announced), PIF should accelerate deployment restart planning. If Scenario 3 rises above 30% (e.g., Iranian strikes on Aramco's Abqaiq processing facility), PIF should immediately activate the crisis contingency reserve.

D2. TASI recovered to within 1% of pre-war close

HIGH CONFIDENCE
The brief states the TASI showed remarkable resilience. This is based on reported trading data through the Eid al-Fitr suspension.
Derivation: The TASI closed at 10,847.93 on February 25 (pre-war). It fell as much as 12% intraday (to ~10,214) in the first days of trading. By March 12, it had recovered to 10,893 — down 2.99% for the month but essentially flat vs. the pre-war close. The final session before the Eid suspension (March 16) closed at 10,886.63. We describe this as "less than 1% below its pre-war close." Bloomberg noted that Aramco's 3.4% gain was the key driver, and that the TASI was the only Gulf exchange still attracting net institutional inflows. Dubai's DFM, by contrast, fell 17% and had not recovered.
House of Saud: How Tadawul Defied the Iran War →  Middle East Insider: TASI Recovery →

D3. Tourism revenue loss of $15–25B annually under Scenario 2

MEDIUM CONFIDENCE
This estimate assumes international arrivals drop 40–60% while domestic and religious tourism partially compensate.
Derivation: Saudi Arabia generated $45B in tourism revenue in 2024 from 30M international visitors. Under Scenario 2, we assume: (a) airspace restrictions and security concerns reduce international leisure arrivals by 50–70%, (b) Hajj/Umrah pilgrimage tourism (which accounts for roughly 30–40% of visitor numbers) declines by only 10–20% (religious obligation is more resilient to conflict than leisure), (c) domestic tourism increases modestly as Saudis substitute international travel. The $15–25B range reflects 33–55% of 2024 tourism revenue, with the wide band reflecting uncertainty about airspace duration and whether Red Sea resort destinations can attract any visitors during active conflict. The Chatham House analysis suggests tourism could be the single most affected non-oil sector.

D4. SAMI World Defense Show contracts: $8.8B

HIGH CONFIDENCE
This is a directly reported figure from the event, confirmed by multiple sources.
Derivation: The World Defense Show 2026, held February 9–13 in Riyadh, concluded with 60 military and defense contracts worth over SAR 33 billion (~$8.8B). This was confirmed by Arab News, Al Defaiya, and the General Authority for Military Industries (GAMI). The event drew 1,486 exhibitors from 89 countries and 137,000 visitors. GAMI Governor Ahmad Al-Ohali noted that defense spending localization had risen from 4% in 2018 to 25% by end-2024. The $8.8B figure represents signed contracts, not aspirational targets — though actual disbursement timelines may extend over multiple years.

E. Structural & Methodological Assumptions

E1. PIF's 15% capital spending cut is acknowledged but actual reallocation may be deeper

MEDIUM CONFIDENCE
The PIF governor's acknowledgment of a 15% cut is reported. Our judgment that the actual figure may be deeper is an inference.
Derivation: House of Saud reported that PIF entered a "wartime footing" — freezing giga-project spending, accelerating SAMI investment, and redirecting agricultural acquisitions for food security. The 15% figure comes from reported comments by PIF leadership. However, when we model the actual capital demands (defense supplementals of $15–25B under Scenario 2, infrastructure repair, food security stockpiling), the implied reallocation is closer to 25–35% of PIF's pre-conflict $57B annual deployment. The gap between the stated 15% and our modeled 25–35% reflects either undisclosed additional spending or a squeeze on future commitments.
House of Saud: PIF Wartime Pivot →

E2. Ecosystem priority ranking is our judgment, not PIF's stated position

LOW CONFIDENCE
The ecosystem ranking in Part VII (Industry & Logistics #1, NEOM #6) represents our analytical recommendation, not PIF's announced strategy. PIF has not publicly re-ranked ecosystems since the conflict began.
Derivation: The ranking is based on: (a) which ecosystems have the most resilient demand signal under conflict (Industry/Logistics benefits from Hormuz disruption; NEOM suffers most), (b) which serve non-discretionary needs (housing is structural; luxury tourism is discretionary), (c) which have dual-use value (defense manufacturing serves both wartime and peacetime needs), and (d) capital efficiency under constrained deployment. This is an opinionated analytical framework, and PIF's leadership may prioritize differently based on political mandates, royal directives, or considerations not visible in public reporting.

E3. FX reserves at $438B provide ~21 months of import cover

HIGH CONFIDENCE
Saudi Arabia's central bank (SAMA) reserves are publicly reported. The import cover calculation is straightforward.
Derivation: SAMA reported net foreign assets of approximately $438B (SAR 1.64T) as of February 2026. With imports running at approximately $210B annually (2025 estimate), this provides roughly 25 months of import cover at pre-conflict rates. Under conflict conditions, imports may decline (reduced construction activity, supply chain disruptions) but defense imports surge. We estimate effective import cover at 18–24 months depending on scenario. The $400B threshold mentioned as a trigger for forced asset sales in Scenario 3 reflects the commonly cited "red line" below which the SAR-USD peg comes under speculative pressure.
